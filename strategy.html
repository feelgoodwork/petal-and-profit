Stage Pre-launch · beta development Market 27,000 US independent florists Model $1,500 setup + $200/mo SaaS Date March 2026 · Confidential
Executive summary
A $135M market with no intelligent profit layer.

There are approximately 27,000 independent retail florists in the United States. The vast majority operate with no real-time visibility into their arrangement-level profitability. They know what they charge. They do not know what they keep — after actual invoice costs, weekly price volatility, and perishable waste.

The enterprise solution to this problem (QuickFlora's ICON AI) is priced at $10,000+ per location — built for grocery chain floral departments, inaccessible to independent shops. The wedding-focused tools (EveryStem, Curate, Details Flowers) solve a different problem entirely. No product currently serves the independent retail florist with live, automated margin intelligence.

Petal & Profit fills this gap. It connects supplier invoices to arrangement recipes and shows live margins — automatically updated, benchmarked against USDA wholesale data, and compatible with any POS the shop already uses. Setup: $1,500. Monthly retainer: $200.

The core thesis
The floral software market is fully served at the high end (enterprise grocery chains) and at the wedding/events niche. The independent retail shop — the owner running a $200K–$2M business, buying from Budzi every week, composting 10–15% of her inventory — has no intelligent profit tool. That is the market. It is large, underserved, and ready.

The gap in one sentence
Every tool in this market either requires manual cost entry, serves a completely different buyer (events vs. retail), or is priced for enterprise operations independent shops cannot afford. The automated, affordable profit intelligence layer for the independent retail florist does not exist.
Positioning

Petal & Profit is not a POS. It is not an events tool. It is the profit intelligence layer that connects to whatever system the shop already runs. Their POS records what happened. Petal & Profit tells them what to do about it.

Why retention is structurally strong
Once a shop's invoices are mapped to their recipes, switching cost is high. Their entire cost history and margin baseline lives in the platform. This is not commodity software — it is built around their specific vendor relationships and arrangement library. Churn at 15% is the conservative assumption; we expect lower once shops see their first week of live margins.

Phase 3 timing note
The Petal Network is not a feature — it is a separate marketplace product with network effects. It requires geographic density (enough shops in a metro) to create real buyer value. Do not build before 30+ active shops. When that density exists, the shops already in the platform are the supply side. The demand side (restaurants, hotels) has no comparable option for same-day fresh stems at a discount.

Go-to-market
How we acquire the first 100 paying shops.
Step 1 — Free demo
Show them their own problem
The UpTowne demo is the primary sales tool. A florist sees real arrangements, real margins, real cost data from a real Ohio shop — and immediately maps it to her own situation. The question after is always "how do I get this with my data?" That is the close.
Step 2 — Wire service audit
Show them the real number
For Dove/FTD Mercury shops: 15-minute audit showing net margin per wire service order after commissions, fees, and membership dues. Most shops have never seen this number. It is often negative on incoming orders. That number sells the retainer.
Step 3 — Beta to case studies
Proof → webinar → pipeline
3–5 beta shops at reduced rate in exchange for documented margin improvement data. Case studies become webinar-to-sales-call funnel content. SAF member lists, regional florist associations, and florist Facebook groups are the distribution channels.

Vertical expansion
The same problem exists in much larger markets.

The core product — connecting supplier invoices to product recipes and showing live margins — is not specific to flowers. The identical problem exists in bakeries, specialty food retail, and restaurants. The architecture is designed to be vertical-agnostic from day one.

Restaurants~660,000 USMenu item cost vs. ingredient invoices. Same unstructured invoice problem. Same margin blindness.Much larger market, existing competitors (MarketMan, BlueCart, Meez). Enter after floral proof.Year 3+ new build
Sequencing recommendation
Win floral completely before pursuing adjacent verticals. The architecture is vertical-agnostic by design — the ingredient → invoice → margin model applies to any perishable business with recipes. But operational focus is the constraint early on. Prove the model at 100+ floral shops, and the restaurant conversation becomes a retrofit with a reference list, not a rebuild from scratch.
